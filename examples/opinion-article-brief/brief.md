# Task Brief — Opinion Article

**Genre:** Opinion article
**Length:** 250–400 words
**Time allotted:** 2 lessons (~100 minutes)
**Expected level:** Lower-intermediate to intermediate (CEFR B1–B2 / IB MYP year 3)
**Rubric:** `rubrics/english-cefr.md`

## Prompt choices

Choose one prompt and write an opinion article giving your view.

- **Prompt A — School and Careers.** "Schools should teach students how to find a job they love, not just how to pass exams." Do you agree?
- **Prompt B — Parents and Dreams.** "It is more important for young people to follow their parents' career advice than to follow their own dreams." Do you agree?
- **Prompt C — AI and the Future.** "In the future, AI will do most jobs. Does this mean humans should stop worrying about careers?"
- **Prompt D — Love and Work.** "Some people say that if you find work you love, it doesn't feel like work at all. Do you agree?"

## Required structural elements

Each article must include all seven of the following. Missing any one triggers the rubric's grade ceiling.

1. **Introduction** — hooks the reader and introduces the topic. A restatement of the prompt is not a hook.
2. **Claim** — states a clear opinion on the topic.
3. **Evidence / Reason 1** — first reason for the position, with an example or explanation.
4. **Evidence / Reason 2** — second, *distinct* reason for the position, with an example or explanation. (Reason 2 paraphrasing Reason 1 does not satisfy this requirement.)
5. **Counterargument** — what someone who disagreed with the student's position might say.
6. **Response** — why the student's opinion is still stronger than the counterargument. The response must directly address the counterargument's reasoning, not restate the claim.
7. **Conclusion** — restates the opinion and offers a final thought. A one-line restatement is not a conclusion.

## Bonus features (not required; credit when present and used purposefully)

- **Rhetorical question** used at an argumentative pivot (not just as a hook). Example: a rhetorical question that introduces the counterargument before the student steel-mans it.
- **Varied sentence openers** used at structural turns: *Although, However, Despite this, On the one hand, On the other hand, In contrast, Therefore, Nevertheless.* Bonus credit when at least three different openers appear and each one sits where the argument turns.
- **A named, specific example** — a real school, country, statistic, study, or person. General claims are weaker than named instances.

## Notes for the marker

- The two-paragraph counterargument-and-response is the most discriminating structural move. Pieces that merge them into a single paragraph often weaken both moves.
- A "concession then rebuttal" pattern — where the student genuinely agrees with part of the counterargument before responding — is the most sophisticated rhetorical move available at this level. Name and credit it when it appears.
- A response that contradicts the student's own claim (e.g. claiming dreams matter, then conceding "but you should choose money") is a serious structural issue. Mark it as such — the piece's argument has fallen apart, regardless of surface fluency.
