# IB-Style Humanities Essay Rubric (1–7)

A holistic rubric for longer humanities essays (history, geography, philosophy, global politics, theory of knowledge, individuals and societies). Modelled loosely on IB Diploma assessment conventions: four criteria, 1–7 scale, holistic synthesis.

This rubric is example-only and not affiliated with the IB Organisation.

---

## Band scale

| Score | Label |
|---|---|
| 1–2 | Limited |
| 3 | Adequate |
| 4 | Competent |
| 5–6 | Strong |
| 7 | Excellent |

One integer per essay. The descriptors describe four tiers (1–2, 3–4, 5–6, 7) but the band labels split 3 from 4 and 5 from 6. Within a tier, pick the higher number if the descriptor is comfortably met, the lower if barely met.

---

## Criteria

### A. Knowledge and Understanding
- **7** — Detailed, accurate, and relevant knowledge demonstrated throughout. Concepts are nuanced and properly contextualised.
- **5–6** — Generally accurate knowledge with good range. Most concepts are understood and contextualised.
- **3–4** — Some accurate knowledge but limited range, with occasional inaccuracies or undeveloped concepts.
- **1–2** — Limited or largely inaccurate knowledge; concepts misunderstood or absent.

### B. Application and Analysis
- **7** — Sophisticated analysis. Sources, evidence, and concepts are weighed against each other. Causation and correlation are kept distinct. Counter-evidence is engaged with seriously.
- **5–6** — Clear analysis with some weighing of competing views. Most claims are supported with evidence.
- **3–4** — Description dominates; analysis is asserted rather than developed. Some attempt to use evidence.
- **1–2** — Mostly descriptive or narrative. Little or no analytical move.

### C. Synthesis and Evaluation
- **7** — A coherent argument that builds across paragraphs and reaches an evaluative judgement supported by what came before. The conclusion adds value, not just summary.
- **5–6** — A consistent argument with a clear evaluative position. Conclusion connects to the argument.
- **3–4** — A position is identifiable but inconsistently maintained. Conclusion is largely restatement.
- **1–2** — No clear position, or position contradicts the body of the essay.

### D. Use of Language
- **7** — Precise vocabulary, including subject-specific terms. Sentence structure varied and accurate. Clearly organised throughout.
- **5–6** — Generally clear and accurate language with some subject-specific vocabulary. Mostly well-organised.
- **3–4** — Language is functional but errors sometimes obscure meaning. Organisation uneven.
- **1–2** — Frequent errors interfere with communication. Organisation unclear.

---

## Synthesis — combining the four into one overall band

Holistic, not arithmetic. The four criteria are weighted roughly equally, but a serious weakness in B (Application and Analysis) or C (Synthesis and Evaluation) typically pulls the overall down more than equivalent weakness in A or D, because the analytical move is the central thing being assessed in humanities essays.

A piece that demonstrates strong knowledge (A=6) but only descriptive use of it (B=3) typically lands at 4, not 5 — the rubric rewards what students *do* with knowledge, not just what they remember.

Apply the inner-split rule: when the synthesis sits between 3 and 4 or between 5 and 6, pick the higher number if the descriptor is comfortably met across most criteria.

---

## Grade-ceiling behavior

If the task brief defines required structural moves (e.g. "must include a thesis, counterargument, and a comparison to a primary source"), missing any one of them caps the overall at **Adequate (3)** for this rubric.


> **Note on spelling.** Unlike the English-CEFR rubric, this one does *not* treat spelling as a separate criterion. Surface accuracy is folded into D (Use of Language), and rewards clear communication over absence of typos. Adjust this metadata field if marking exam scripts where spelling is explicitly weighted.
