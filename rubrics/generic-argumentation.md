# Generic Argumentation Rubric (1–4)

A short, subject-agnostic rubric for any piece of writing where the student is making an argument. Designed to be readable by a student as well as a teacher, with a 4-point scale and three criteria.

Use this rubric when you want a lightweight assessment that ports across subjects — debate brief, position paper, persuasive essay, opinion piece, op-ed style writing in a humanities class, philosophy short response, history "to what extent" question. It does not assume any particular text-type conventions or language profile.

---

## Band scale

| Score | Label |
|---|---|
| 1 | Beginning |
| 2 | Developing |
| 3 | Proficient |
| 4 | Mastery |

One integer per piece. No half-bands.

---

## Criteria

### 1. Claim and Position
- **4** — A clear, original, defensible position is stated early and held throughout. The claim is precise (not "X is good" but "X is good for Y, because Z").
- **3** — A clear position is stated and largely held throughout.
- **2** — A position is identifiable but vague, or shifts during the piece.
- **1** — No clear position, or the position contradicts the rest of the piece.

### 2. Reasons and Evidence
- **4** — Two or more distinct reasons, each supported by specific evidence (a named example, a fact, a quotation, a worked case). Counter-evidence is acknowledged and addressed.
- **3** — Two distinct reasons, each with at least one supporting example. Some engagement with opposing views.
- **2** — Reasons are present but undeveloped, or two reasons that restate each other. Evidence is asserted rather than shown.
- **1** — No reasons given, or reasons that do not connect to the claim.

### 3. Communication
- **4** — The writing reads cleanly. Vocabulary is precise. Sentences are well-formed. Organisation is logical and aids the argument. Errors do not impede meaning.
- **3** — Mostly clear and well-organised. Some errors, but meaning is not lost.
- **2** — Some clarity but errors or organisation issues regularly interrupt meaning.
- **1** — Errors or disorganisation prevent the reader from following the argument.

---

## Synthesis

The overall band is a holistic combination of the three. None of the criteria dominates.

- 4-4-4 → 4.
- 3-3-3 → 3.
- 4-3-2 → 3 (the floor of two strong scores against one weak one usually lands in the middle).
- 4-4-2 → 3 (a 2 in any criterion is significant enough to weigh the overall down).
- 4-2-2 → 2.

A 1 in any criterion typically caps the overall at 2.

---

## Grade-ceiling behavior

If the task brief defines required structural moves and one is absent, the overall is capped at **Developing (2)**. (Note: this rubric uses a lower cap than the 1–7 rubrics, because the 1–4 scale has less room — a band-2 cap on a 4-point scale is the equivalent of band-3 on a 7-point scale.)

> **Subject portability.** This rubric does not name any subject-specific conventions (no "text-type", no "register"). It can be used as-is for English, history, philosophy, science write-ups where an argument is being made, or any class where you want a fast common-language rubric across departments.
